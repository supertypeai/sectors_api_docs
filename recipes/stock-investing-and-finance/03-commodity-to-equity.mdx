## Summary

Mining stocks on IDX (think coal names like ADRO, PTBA, and ITMG, or nickel names like ANTM, INCO, and NCKL) do not move mainly because of the same things that move a bank or a consumer goods company. Their earnings are driven, in large part, by something that happens far outside the company: the world price of the commodity they dig up and sell.

This recipe uses the **Sectors API v2 Mining API**, a data source that (as of this writing) has not yet been covered by any other Sectors recipe, and links it directly to IDX equity data. You will:

1. Discover which commodities Sectors has price history for, and pull that history
2. Map a set of commodities to the IDX-listed mining companies most exposed to them
3. Pull each company's quarterly revenue and earnings, and each commodity's price history
4. Line the two series up by quarter and compute a simple **"commodity beta"**, a rough sensitivity score for how much a company's earnings tend to move when the underlying commodity price moves
5. Confirm the story using mining-specific fundamentals (production volume, revenue breakdown by commodity) instead of relying on the stock price alone
6. Add national-level context (total production, export destinations) so you understand the supply side too

**Who this is for:** Analysts and content creators covering IDX mining and energy names, who want to go beyond "coal prices are up, so ADRO should be up" and actually check the relationship with real data.

**What you'll produce:** A Python notebook that ranks IDX mining stocks by commodity sensitivity, a chart overlaying commodity price against company earnings, and a small reusable toolkit for pulling and chunking Mining API data.

<Warning>
  This recipe is for education and content purposes only. It is not financial advice. Commodity prices, exchange rates, hedging policies, and company-specific costs all affect real-world results, and a statistical relationship in the past does not guarantee it will hold in the future. Always do your own research before making an investment decision.
</Warning>

## Why This Matters

The Sectors Mining API covers company-level operating data (licenses, sites, production, financials) and market-level commodity data (prices, national production, export destinations) that simply does not exist in a typical equity data feed. Most public discussion of "commodity plays" on IDX stays qualitative: an article says nickel prices fell, so nickel miners should have had a bad quarter, without ever actually checking the size of the effect or which company was hit hardest.

Two real, publicly reported examples show why this matters. In one recent year, ANTM's quarterly net profit reportedly dropped very sharply year-on-year, and public reporting tied that mainly to a steep fall in nickel ore sales revenue that quarter. In a separate year, ITMG's annual net profit reportedly fell by close to half, and public reporting tied that mainly to a lower average selling price for coal, even though sales volume actually increased. Both stories are consistent with the same underlying idea: for a mining company, the price of the commodity it sells can matter more to profit than how much it actually produces.

Rather than repeating headlines like these, this recipe shows you how to pull the live Sectors data yourself and check the relationship directly, for whichever commodities and companies you care about right now.

<Note>
  Cross-table Mining API coverage (production, exports, reserves, sites) is currently limited to **Coal**, **Gold**, **Nickel**, and **Copper**, with partial data for Silver, Cobalt, and Bauxite. Commodity _price_ history has broader coverage. Always call the commodities list endpoint first to check what is actually available before you build a workflow around a specific commodity.
</Note>

### Step 2: Confirm the IDX Ticker Behind Each Mining Company

Mining company records in Sectors are indexed by `slug` (for example `pt-adaro-andalan-indonesia-tbk`), not by IDX ticker. Some slugs correspond directly to a listed company (the detail endpoint returns a `symbol` field), while others are private subsidiaries whose listed _parent_ is one level up in the ownership tree. This helper checks both places instead of assuming.

```python
def find_mining_companies(keyword):
    """Search the Mining API by company name, symbol, or key operation keyword."""
    url = f"{BASE_URL}/mining/companies/"
    r = requests.get(url, headers=headers, params={"keyword": keyword, "limit": 10})
    r.raise_for_status()
    return r.json()["results"]


def resolve_slug_and_symbol(keyword):
    """
    Search for a mining company by keyword, then confirm the IDX ticker it
    (or its listed parent) trades under. Returns (slug, symbol) or (None, None)
    if nothing usable was found.
    """
    candidates = find_mining_companies(keyword)
    if not candidates:
        return None, None

    slug = candidates[0]["slug"]

    detail = requests.get(f"{BASE_URL}/mining/companies/{slug}/", headers=headers).json()
    if detail.get("symbol"):
        return slug, detail["symbol"]

    # Not listed itself, check the ownership tree for a listed parent
    ownership = requests.get(f"{BASE_URL}/mining/companies/ownership/{slug}/", headers=headers).json()
    for parent in ownership.get("parents", []):
        if parent.get("symbol"):
            return slug, parent["symbol"]

    return slug, None


# Example: confirm the slug and ticker for one company before trusting the whole map
slug, symbol = resolve_slug_and_symbol("adaro")
print(f"slug={slug}, resolved symbol={symbol}")
```

### Step 3: Pull Commodity Price History (Chunked)

The [Commodity Price History](https://docs.sectors.app/api-references/v2/mining/commodities-trade/commodity-price) endpoint caps every request at a **3-year window**. To build a longer history for a proper sensitivity study, chunk the request, the same pattern used for the daily transaction endpoint elsewhere in these recipes.

```python
def get_commodity_price_history(commodity_name, start_year, end_year):
    """
    /v2/mining/commodities/{commodity_name}/price/ caps each request at 3 years.
    This walks a longer range in 3-year chunks and concatenates the results.
    """
    frames = []
    cursor = start_year
    url = f"{BASE_URL}/mining/commodities/{commodity_name}/price/"

    while cursor <= end_year:
        chunk_end = min(cursor + 2, end_year)
        r = requests.get(url, headers=headers, params={"start_year": cursor, "end_year": chunk_end})
        if r.status_code == 200:
            frames.append(pd.DataFrame(r.json()))
        time.sleep(0.2)
        cursor = chunk_end + 1

    if not frames:
        return pd.DataFrame()
    prices = pd.concat(frames, ignore_index=True).drop_duplicates(subset="date")
    prices["date"] = pd.to_datetime(prices["date"])
    return prices.sort_values("date")
```

### Step 5: Align Commodity Price to Company Earnings, by Quarter

Commodity prices in Sectors are monthly (or bi-weekly for the most recent coal entries); company financials are quarterly. Resample the commodity series to a quarterly average, then merge on the calendar quarter.

```python
def to_quarterly_average(price_df, value_col):
    df = price_df.copy()
    df["year_quarter"] = pd.PeriodIndex(df["date"], freq="Q")
    return df.groupby("year_quarter")[value_col].mean().reset_index()


def build_sensitivity_table(company_df, commodity_df, commodity_value_col="price_usd_per_ton"):
    """
    Merge a company's quarterly financials with a commodity's quarterly average
    price, then compute year-over-year change for both series so the units
    are comparable (percent growth vs. percent growth, not rupiah vs. dollars).
    """
    company = company_df.copy()
    company["year_quarter"] = pd.PeriodIndex(company["date"], freq="Q")

    commodity_q = to_quarterly_average(commodity_df, commodity_value_col)

    merged = company.merge(commodity_q, on="year_quarter", how="inner").sort_values("year_quarter")
    merged["commodity_price_yoy"] = merged[commodity_value_col].pct_change(4)
    merged["earnings_yoy"] = merged["earnings"].pct_change(4)
    merged["revenue_yoy"] = merged["revenue"].pct_change(4)
    return merged
```

### Step 6: Compute a Simple "Commodity Beta" for Each Company

A commodity beta above 1 means the company's earnings tend to swing by _more_ than the commodity price does. A number close to 0 means earnings barely react to the commodity at all, which could mean strong hedging, a diversified revenue base, or simply a weak real-world link.

```python
def compute_commodity_beta(sensitivity_df, x_col="commodity_price_yoy", y_col="earnings_yoy"):
    """
    Fit a simple straight line (y = slope * x + intercept) between commodity
    price growth and earnings growth. The slope is the "commodity beta".
    Returns None if there are not enough clean data points to fit a line.
    """
    clean = sensitivity_df.dropna(subset=[x_col, y_col])
    if len(clean) < 4:
        return None
    slope, intercept = np.polyfit(clean[x_col], clean[y_col], 1)
    correlation = clean[x_col].corr(clean[y_col])
    return {"beta": round(slope, 2), "correlation": round(correlation, 2), "n_quarters": len(clean)}


result = compute_commodity_beta(itmg_sensitivity)
print(f"ITMG commodity beta vs. coal price: {result}")
```

Now loop this across your whole map to build a ranking table.

```python
def build_beta_ranking(commodity_equity_map, start_year=2019, end_year=2026):
    rows = []
    price_cache = {}

    for commodity, symbols in commodity_equity_map.items():
        commodity_key = commodity.lower()
        if commodity_key not in price_cache:
            price_cache[commodity_key] = get_commodity_price_history(commodity_key, start_year, end_year)
        commodity_df = price_cache[commodity_key]
        if commodity_df.empty:
            continue

        for symbol in symbols:
            quarterly = get_quarterly_financials(symbol, n_quarters=24)
            time.sleep(0.2)
            if quarterly.empty:
                continue
            sensitivity = build_sensitivity_table(quarterly, commodity_df)
            beta_result = compute_commodity_beta(sensitivity)
            if beta_result is None:
                continue
            rows.append({"symbol": symbol, "commodity": commodity, **beta_result})

    return pd.DataFrame(rows).sort_values("beta", ascending=False)


beta_ranking = build_beta_ranking(COMMODITY_EQUITY_MAP)
```

### Step 7: Confirm With Mining-Side Fundamentals

A stock-based commodity beta can be noisy. Confirm the story using the company's actual operating data: production volume and, where available, its revenue breakdown by segment.

```python
def get_mining_financials(slug, year=None):
    """
    Annual financials (in USD millions) from the Mining API, for the
    company's mining operations specifically, not the IDX-listed group
    as a whole if they differ.
    """
    url = f"{BASE_URL}/mining/companies/financials/{slug}/"
    params = {"year": year} if year else {}
    r = requests.get(url, headers=headers, params=params)
    if r.status_code != 200:
        return None
    return r.json()


def get_mining_performance(slug, commodity_type=None, year=None):
    """Production volume, sales volume, and reserves for a mining company."""
    url = f"{BASE_URL}/mining/companies/performance/{slug}/"
    params = {}
    if commodity_type:
        params["commodity_type"] = commodity_type
    if year:
        params["year"] = year
    r = requests.get(url, headers=headers, params=params)
    if r.status_code != 200:
        return None
    return r.json()


def get_revenue_segments(symbol, financial_year=None):
    """
    Revenue broken down by source (for example, 'Coal (Export)' vs. 'Logistics').
    Useful to check how much of a company's revenue is truly commodity-driven,
    versus, say, trading or services income that would dilute the sensitivity.
    """
    url = f"{BASE_URL}/company/get-segments/{symbol}/"
    params = {"financial_year": financial_year} if financial_year else {}
    r = requests.get(url, headers=headers, params=params)
    if r.status_code != 200:
        return None
    return r.json()
```

## How to Interpret Your Results

- **A high commodity beta with a high correlation** is the strongest case for "this stock's earnings really do move with the commodity." Treat a high beta with a _low_ correlation more carefully, since it may be driven by one or two noisy quarters rather than a real pattern.
- **Compare the stock-level beta against the revenue segment breakdown from Step 7.** If a company's beta looks low but its segment data shows over 80% of revenue coming directly from the commodity, look for another explanation, such as hedging contracts, currency effects, or a cost structure that also moves with the commodity (so profit is more stable than revenue).
- **Use the national production and export data from Step 8 as a sanity check**, not a substitute for company data. A commodity's national production falling does not automatically mean every producer's output fell by the same amount; check the individual company's own performance data before assuming.
- **Small `n_quarters` samples produce noisy betas.** Twenty-four quarters (six years) is a reasonable starting point; fewer than twelve quarters should be treated as a rough first look, not a final answer.

## Common Mistakes

**Assuming a mining company's slug is also its IDX ticker.** Many mining entities in the Mining API are private subsidiaries. Always resolve the slug to a symbol with `resolve_slug_and_symbol`, and check the ownership tree if the `symbol` field on the company detail comes back empty.

**Requesting more than 3 years of commodity price history in one call.** The API returns a 400 error above that range. Always chunk with `get_commodity_price_history`.

**Mixing IDR and USD without converting.** Equity financials from `/v2/financials/quarterly/` are in IDR; Mining API financials from `/v2/mining/companies/financials/{slug}/` are in USD millions. Comparing year-over-year percentage changes (as this recipe does) sidesteps the currency issue, but if you ever compare absolute values side by side, convert them to the same currency first.

**Forgetting the credit cost of quarterly financials.** This endpoint costs 1 API credit per quarter returned, so pulling `n_quarters=24` for ten companies is 240 credits in one notebook run. Start with a smaller `n_quarters` while developing your code, and only scale up once the logic is confirmed.

**Treating correlation as causation.** A strong statistical relationship between a commodity price and a company's earnings does not rule out a third factor (for example, a broader commodities supercycle affecting the whole market, or a shared currency effect) driving both at once.

## Extensions

**Extend to Copper and Silver.** The Mining API's cross-table coverage includes Copper alongside Coal, Gold, and Nickel. Add copper-exposed names and re-run the same pipeline.

**Add a currency overlay.** Pull USD/IDR exchange rate data from a separate source and add it as a third series in Step 9's chart, since a weaker rupiah can offset (or amplify) a falling USD-denominated commodity price for an exporter.

**Turn the beta ranking into a screener.** Combine `beta_ranking` with the [Companies Screener](https://docs.sectors.app/api-references/v2/indonesia/screener/companies)'s `where` clause (for example, filtering on `pe < pe_peer_avg`) to find mining stocks that look statistically cheap relative to peers _and_ have a confirmed link to a commodity you have a view on.

**Build a live dashboard.** Wire the functions in this recipe into a Streamlit app, following the same pattern as the [SectorScan recipe](/recipes/build-python-app/sectorscan/01-sectorscan-part1), so the beta ranking refreshes automatically whenever new quarterly financials or commodity prices land.

**Widen the ownership check.** Use the [Mining Company Ownership](https://docs.sectors.app/api-references/v2/mining/companies/mining-companies-ownership) endpoint recursively (parents of parents) for group structures with more than one holding layer, common among the larger Indonesian mining conglomerates.

- [IDX Fact Book](https://www.idx.co.id) — Indonesia Stock Exchange annual reference for listed company data
